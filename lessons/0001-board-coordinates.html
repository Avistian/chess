The Board and Coordinates

Lesson 001 · ~10 minutes

Every tournament game is recorded in algebraic notation — a compact language for naming squares and moves. Before tactics, openings, or endgames, you need to read the board fluently. This is the skill players skip, and it slows everything else down.

The grid

A chessboard is 8 squares wide and 8 squares tall — 64 squares total. Two labels define every square:

  • Files — the vertical columns, labeled a through h from left to right (White's perspective).
  • Ranks — the horizontal rows, labeled 1 through 8 from bottom to top (White's perspective).

A square's name is its file letter + rank number. The bottom-left square (from White's view) is a1. The center square where many games begin is e4 (White's king pawn two squares forward).

Tournament tip: Sit with the board oriented so each player has a light square on their right-hand corner ("light on the right"). White's pieces start on ranks 1 and 2; Black's on ranks 7 and 8.

How moves are written

A move names the destination square, not the path. Examples:

  • e4 — a pawn moves to e4
  • Nf3 — the knight moves to f3 (piece letter + square; N = Knight, B = Bishop, R = Rook, Q = Queen, K = King)
  • exd5 — the pawn on the e-file captures on d5
  • O-O — kingside castling

In a tournament you'll write these on a scoresheet move-by-move. Being fast at finding square names saves time and prevents illegal-move disputes.

Practice — find the square

Click the square named in the prompt. You need 8 out of 10 to pass. Run it twice if you miss — retrieval practice is the point.
