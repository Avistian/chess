Tactics Introduction

Lesson 004 · ~12 minutes · You: 415 Elo · forks, pins, loose pieces

You know how to finish a won endgame. Now learn how games become won in the first place. At 415 Elo, most decisive moments are not deep combinations — they are one-move oversights: a piece left undefended, a knight fork, a bishop pin. Phase 1 study is roughly 70% tactics (study roadmap). This lesson names the three patterns you will see most often.

One idea: scan for loose pieces

Before you calculate a long line, ask one question: does my opponent have a piece nobody defends? A loose piece (also called a hanging or undefended piece) is free material — take it. Forks and pins often create loose pieces by forcing a king to move while something else stays behind.

Tournament habit: On every opponent move, glance at what that move attacked and what it left behind. Most blunders at your level are hanging pieces, not missed mate-in-four.

Fork — one piece, two targets

A fork is a single move that attacks two or more enemy pieces at once. The defender can only save one. Knights are the classic forkers — they jump in an L-shape and ignore blockers.

Queens and pawns fork too, but knights show up in almost every beginner game. When you see a knight one jump away from the enemy king, check what else sits on its landing squares.

Pin — piece frozen on a line

A pin ties a piece to something valuable behind it on a straight line (file, rank, or diagonal). A relative pin to a queen or rook is painful; an absolute pin to the king is worse — the pinned piece cannot legally move.

Pins are slow pressure. You pile up on the pinned piece, or use the pin to win tempo elsewhere. Bishops and rooks pin most often; queens pin along any line.

Loose piece — take what's free

Not every tactic is flashy. If a rook sits on a square with zero defenders, your queen or bishop may simply capture it. At your rating, this happens constantly — players chase attacks and forget to defend.

The scan order that works at your level: loose pieces → checks that fork → pins you can exploit. Save discovered attacks and skewers for after these feel automatic.

Practice — tactic recognition

Eight positions: name the tactic or pick the best move. You need 6 out of 8 to pass. Every answer choice is the same length — no hints from formatting.

Real-world drill

After this quiz, open Lichess Practice and run Fork and Pin until you pass without hints. Then add 15 minutes of daily puzzles at Lichess Training — this is the single highest- leverage habit on your path to 1400.
