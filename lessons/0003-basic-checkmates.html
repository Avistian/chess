Basic Checkmates

Lesson 003 · ~12 minutes · You: 415 Elo, first win

You won your first rated game — that matters. Most beginners never convert a winning position; they stalemate, blunder the queen, or run out of time. At 415 Elo you are still in Phase 1 (foundation), but you have proof you can finish a game. This lesson teaches the two mates you will see most often when you are ahead: queen + king and rook + king vs a lone king.

One idea: shrink the box

A lone king can be trapped on the board. Imagine a rectangle of squares the enemy king is allowed to occupy. Your job is to make that rectangle smaller until the king is squeezed to the edge or corner — then deliver checkmate.

  • Queen cuts off files, ranks, and diagonals at a distance — fastest boxer.
  • Rook cuts off one file or rank — the ladder mate pattern.
  • Your king walks in only after the enemy king is boxed — two kings must not touch.
Conversion rule: When you are winning, ask every move: does my opponent still have a legal move? A careless queen move can stalemate and turn a win into a draw.

Queen + king vs king

Place the queen one knight-move away from the enemy king (not adjacent — that can stalemate on the edge). Each queen move should chop off a row or column of escape squares. Your king follows at a safe distance until you can deliver mate on the back rank or in a corner.

You do not need to memorize every move order. You need the habit: restrict first, check second. Wild checks let the king run toward the center and waste time.

Rook + king vs king (ladder mate)

The rook cuts off a rank or file. Each rook check (or quiet rook slide) pushes the enemy king one row closer to the edge. Your king follows on the same file or rank until the rook delivers mate on the back rank or a-file.

Two rooks vs king is even easier (alternating checks along ranks). One rook takes more patience — the same boxing idea applies.

Check vs checkmate vs stalemate

Before you play the “winning” move, scan every square around the enemy king. Tournament players at 2000 still occasionally stalemate a won queen ending.

Practice — mate recognition

Eight positions: mate, near-miss, or best move. You need 6 out of 8 to pass. Every answer choice is the same length — no hints from formatting.

Real-world drill

After this quiz, open Lichess Practice → Checkmates and run Queen vs King and Rook vs King until you can finish both without hints. That is the skill that turns your next +queen position into a win instead of a draw.
