Rules Refresh & Special Moves

Lesson 002 · ~12 minutes

Tournament arbiters enforce rules you may not see in casual online blitz. Players near 2000 do not lose games because they forgot how a knight moves — they lose points on castling, en passant, stalemate, and touch-move. This lesson locks in the edge cases before tactics work.

Check, checkmate, stalemate

In tournaments, stalemate saves many lost positions. Before you queen a pawn, ask: does the opponent have any legal move? See Chess Stack Exchange — rules for precise wording when something odd happens at the board.

Castling (O-O / O-O-O)

A one-time king move: the king slides two squares toward a rook; the rook jumps to the other side. Written O-O (kingside) or O-O-O (queenside).

Castling is illegal if any of these are true:

  • The king is currently in check
  • The king would cross or land on an attacked square
  • The king or that rook has already moved
  • There are pieces between king and rook
Tournament tip: A rook under attack may still castle — only the king's path must be safe. Many players get this wrong under time pressure.

En passant

When an enemy pawn advances two squares from its starting rank and lands beside your pawn, you may capture it as if it moved one square — but only on the very next move.

Promotion

A pawn reaching the far rank promotes to queen, rook, bishop, or knight (your choice). Under-promotion to a knight can deliver immediate checkmate; promoting to a king is never legal.
