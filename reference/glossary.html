Chess Glossary

Quick reference · updated as lessons progress

Algebraic notation
Standard system for recording moves. Squares named by file + rank (e.g. e4). Pieces by letter: K, Q, R, B, N.
File
Vertical column of squares. Labeled a–h from White's left.
Rank
Horizontal row of squares. Labeled 1–8 from White's side.
Check
The king is under attack and must escape on the next move.
Checkmate
The king is in check and has no legal escape. Game over.
Boxing (the king)
Restricting the enemy king to a shrinking rectangle of squares until it is trapped on the edge or in a corner.
Ladder mate
Rook (or rooks) cutting off ranks or files to push the enemy king to the back rank, then mating with king support.
Conversion
Turning a winning advantage (extra material, better position) into a full point instead of a draw or loss.
Stalemate
Side to move has no legal move but is not in check. Game is a draw.
Castling
One-time king move: king slides two squares toward a rook; rook jumps to the other side. Kingside (O-O) or queenside (O-O-O).
En passant
Special pawn capture when an opponent pawn advances two squares and lands beside your pawn.
Elo / Rating
Numeric estimate of playing strength. 2000 ≈ strong club / expert level on most online platforms.
Tactic
Short combination exploiting a concrete opportunity — fork, pin, skewer, discovered attack, etc.
Fork
One move attacks two or more enemy pieces at once. The defender can only save one. Knights are the classic forkers.
Pin
A piece on a line cannot move without exposing a more valuable piece behind it — usually the king (absolute pin) or queen/rook (relative pin).
Loose piece
An enemy piece with no defender on its square. Also called hanging or undefended. Often the simplest tactic — just capture it.
Touch-move
If you touch a piece you can legally move, you must move it. Say "j'adoube" before adjusting a piece without moving it.
Promotion
When a pawn reaches the far rank, it becomes a queen, rook, bishop, or knight (never a king).
Draw
Game ends with neither player winning. Common causes: stalemate, insufficient material, agreement, threefold repetition, 50-move rule.
Opening repertoire
The set of openings you play regularly as White and Black. At your level, narrow and understood beats wide and memorized.
